Akwesasne

Location: St. Lawrence River | Era: 1750–Present

Akwesasne, meaning "Land Where the Partridge Drums," is a vital Mohawk territory that uniquely straddles the modern borders of New York, Ontario, and Quebec. Established as a Jesuit mission in the mid-18th century, it quickly evolved into a primary population center. Its people were master river navigators, utilizing the St. Lawrence for extensive trade, fishing, and diplomatic missions that sustained the community through centuries of colonial pressure and modern environmental and political challenges.

Canadaseago

Location: Near Geneva, NY | Era: 1700–1779

Known as the "Seneca Castle," Canadaseago was the grand 18th-century capital of the Seneca Nation. This sophisticated settlement featured over sixty large, well-built longhouses, extensive apple and peach orchards, and thousands of acres of corn and beans. As the political heart of the Western Door, it hosted crucial diplomatic councils between the Haudenosaunee and the British Empire. During the 1779 Sullivan Expedition, American forces systematically burned the entire village and all surrounding food sources to break Seneca resistance.

Chemung

Location: Chemung River | Era: 1750–1779

Chemung was a prosperous village shared by Cayuga and Seneca families on the banks of the Chemung River. The name refers to a "Big Horn," inspired by a mammoth tusk found in the riverbank. This settlement was a critical agricultural center, providing massive amounts of food for warriors during the Revolutionary War. It served as a strategic scouting post for the British-Iroquois alliance until it was destroyed by the American Continental Army during a fierce engagement in the late summer of 1779.

Conewango

Location: Warren, PA | Era: 1700–1779

Conewango sat at the vital confluence of the Allegheny River and Conewango Creek, serving as the Seneca’s southwestern frontier post. It functioned as a strategic gateway to the Ohio Valley, allowing the Seneca to monitor river traffic and maintain complex diplomatic relationships with the Shawnee and Delaware tribes. Surrounded by miles of productive cornfields, it served as a major hunting base until it was leveled by the Broadhead Expedition, which mirrored the scorched-earth tactics used in the northern campaigns.

Coreorgonel

Location: Ithaca, NY | Era: 1750–1779

Coreorgonel was a distinctive settlement founded by the Tutelo people, a Siouan-speaking group who fled northward from North Carolina and Virginia to seek protection under the Great Law of Peace. Formally adopted into the Cayuga Nation, the Tutelo established this village of twenty-five large houses near the southern tip of Cayuga Lake. The settlement served as a living testament to Haudenosaunee humanitarian principles, providing a home for displaced peoples until it was completely destroyed by American forces in 1779.

Ganondagan

Location: Victor, NY | Era: 1300–1687

Ganondagan was the "Town of Peace" and the 17th-century capital of the Seneca Nation. It was a sprawling, unfortified settlement, representing a time of peak Seneca power and confidence. It contained over 150 longhouses and a nearby granary on Boughton Hill that stored millions of bushels of grain. The village was a major hub for Dutch trade until 1687, when a French invasion force under the Marquis de Denonville burned the town and its food stores to the ground.

Hochelaga

Location: Montreal Island | Era: 1450–1540

Hochelaga was a large, fortified St. Lawrence Iroquoian village visited by Jacques Cartier in 1535. The village was circular and protected by a triple row of wooden palisades, housing over fifty longhouses and approximately 3,000 residents. Located at the foot of Mount Royal, its people practiced intensive corn agriculture. By the time French explorers returned decades later, the village had completely disappeared, likely due to a combination of European diseases and warfare with the rising Five Nations Confederacy.

Kahnawake

Location: St. Lawrence South Shore | Era: 1667–Present

Kahnawake, meaning "At the Rapids," was founded by Mohawk families who moved toward the St. Lawrence River to maintain their autonomy and engage in trade between French and British interests. It became a powerful center for the Catholic Mohawk community and a significant military force in North America. The community played a key role in protecting the St. Lawrence trade routes and has maintained a fierce dedication to Mohawk sovereignty and cultural preservation for over three centuries.

Kanehsatake

Location: Lake of Two Mountains | Era: 1721–Present

Kanehsatake was established as a multi-ethnic settlement where Mohawk, Algonquin, and Nipissing peoples coexisted at the confluence of the Ottawa and St. Lawrence rivers. Historically governed by the Sulpician Order under French land grants, the community has a long and turbulent history of land disputes and resistance against colonial authority. The village served as a key point of control for the fur trade moving down the Ottawa River and has remained a bastion of Indigenous resistance into the modern era.

Kanatsiohare

Location: Mohawk Valley, NY | Era: 1730–1779

Kanatsiohare, or "The Place of the Clean Pot," was a significant Mohawk village and the ancestral home of the prominent Brant family, including the leader Joseph Brant. It was a "Lower Mohawk" castle that showcased the blending of traditional longhouse structures with colonial architectural influences. The village was a center for Mohawk education and culture until the American Revolution, when the inhabitants were forced to flee to Canada as the Mohawk Valley was ravaged by civil war and colonial militia raids.

Kente

Location: Quinte Peninsula | Era: 1668–1680

Kente was a Cayuga settlement established on the northern shore of Lake Ontario during a period of Haudenosaunee northward expansion. The village hosted a Sulpician mission and served as a strategic base for Cayuga hunting and fishing in the newly acquired northern territories. Although the settlement was relatively short-lived due to shifting regional alliances and the depletion of local fur resources, it remains an important historical marker of the massive geographic reach of the Haudenosaunee in the late 17th century.

Little Beard’s Town

Location: Cuylerville, NY | Era: 1750–1779

Also known as Chenussio, this was the largest Seneca village in the Genesee Valley, consisting of 128 longhouses and home to the warrior Little Beard. The village was famous for its massive agricultural output, including vast orchards and cornfields that fed the entire Western Door. Because of its fame and size, it was chosen as the ultimate target for the Sullivan Expedition. American soldiers spent several days systematically burning every house and cutting down thousands of fruit trees to ensure the area was uninhabitable.

Minisink

Location: Delaware River | Era: 1650–1779

Minisink was a vital frontier village inhabited by Munsee Delaware and Haudenosaunee people. Located at a major crossing of the Delaware River, it served as a primary hub for the regional fur trade and diplomacy between the Iroquois and the middle colonies. The village acted as a crucial cultural crossroads until 1779, when Joseph Brant led a raid against the settlement, resulting in the Battle of Minisink. The ensuing violence and colonial retaliation eventually led to the permanent displacement of its inhabitants.

Newtown

Location: Elmira, NY | Era: 1770–1779

Newtown was a strategic Seneca and Delaware village that became the site of the only major pitched battle of the Sullivan Expedition. Here, a combined force of Iroquois warriors and British rangers attempted to halt the American advance. Following their defeat, the village—and the vast food stores meant to sustain the Confederacy through the winter—were destroyed. The loss at Newtown triggered a massive refugee crisis, as thousands were forced to flee toward the British at Fort Niagara to escape starvation.

Oquaga

Location: Susquehanna River | Era: 1700–1778

Oquaga was a highly diverse and prosperous town on the upper Susquehanna River, serving as a melting pot for Mohawk, Oneida, Tuscarora, and Mahican peoples. It functioned as a major diplomatic "gateway" to the southern colonies and featured European-style frame houses, apple orchards, and blacksmith shops. Because it served as a primary staging ground for Loyalist and Iroquois raids against the American frontier, it was targeted and burned by American Continental forces in October 1778, marking the beginning of the end for the Susquehanna settlements.

Oriska

Location: Oriskany, NY | Era: 1750–1777

Oriska was a significant Oneida village located near the critical "Oneida Carry" portage. Unlike many other nations in the Confederacy, the Oneida of Oriska were deeply divided in their loyalties during the American Revolution, with many eventually siding with the American rebels. The village was situated near the site of the bloody Battle of Oriskany in 1777, a tragic conflict that pitted Haudenosaunee warriors against one another and effectively shattered the centuries-old "Great Law of Peace" that had prevented internal civil war.

Ossernenon

Location: Auriesville, NY | Era: 1640–1666

Ossernenon was a heavily fortified Mohawk "castle" famous for its role in the early colonial wars of the 17th century. It was the site where several French Jesuit missionaries were held captive and the birthplace of Kateri Tekakwitha, who later became the first Native American saint. The village was a symbol of Mohawk power in the valley until a massive French military expedition in 1666 burned the palisades and longhouses, forcing the Mohawk to move their settlements to more defensible positions north of the river.

Oswegatchie

Location: Ogdensburg, NY | Era: 1748–1800

Oswegatchie was established as a French-led mission settlement specifically for Onondaga and Cayuga families who wished to remain neutral or aligned with French interests. It grew into a powerful autonomous settlement known as the "Fourteen Nations of Oswegatchie." The village provided the Iroquois with a strategic northern point of influence, allowing them to monitor trade and military movements along the eastern reaches of Lake Ontario and the St. Lawrence River before the community was eventually pressured to relocate after the British conquest.

Quinaouatoua

Location: Near Hamilton, ON | Era: 1660–1690

Quinaouatoua was a Seneca "outpost" village strategically positioned near the western end of Lake Ontario. It served as a vital portage point for Seneca hunting parties and warriors moving between the Great Lakes. The village was visited by early French explorers such as La Salle and stood as a clear testament to the Seneca's ability to project their sovereign power far beyond their traditional homelands in modern-day New York. It was eventually abandoned as Seneca interests shifted back to the protection of their southern heartland territories.

Schaghticoke

Location: Hudson River | Era: 1675–1750

Schaghticoke was a unique refugee settlement established under the protection of the Haudenosaunee and the colonial government of New York. It was populated by Mahican and New England tribes, such as the Abenaki, who had been displaced following the devastation of King Philip’s War. The Iroquois referred to these residents as their "children," providing them military protection in exchange for the village serving as a strategic buffer zone against northern invasions. The community eventually dispersed as its members migrated to Mohawk mission towns in Canada.

Stadacona

Location: Quebec City | Era: 1450–1545

Stadacona was the major St. Lawrence Iroquoian village located where Quebec City stands today. Led by the influential Chief Donnacona, the residents first encountered Jacques Cartier in 1534. The village was a thriving hub for fishing and agriculture but suffered catastrophic population losses from European-borne diseases and harsh winters. Like its sister city Hochelaga, the village disappeared from the historical record by the late 16th century, with its survivors likely being absorbed into the Huron-Wendat or the growing Five Nations Iroquois Confederacy.

Teiaiagon

Location: Toronto, ON | Era: 1660–1687

Teiaiagon was a Seneca village located on the banks of the Humber River in present-day Toronto. It was an essential center for the northern fur trade, situated at the southern end of the "Toronto Carrying Place" trail. This overland path allowed traders to bypass the difficult Niagara Falls route to reach the upper Great Lakes. The village was a bustling multicultural site until it was abandoned in 1687 following French military attacks and the subsequent consolidation of Seneca power back in their southern Finger Lakes territories.

Totiakton

Location: Honeoye Falls, NY | Era: 1600–1687

Totiakton was one of the four principal "castles" of the Seneca Nation during the mid-17th century. As an unfortified town, its lack of palisades reflected the immense military confidence and regional dominance of the Seneca at the time. The village was home to several thousand people and served as a major center for high-level diplomacy with French and Dutch colonial officials. Like Ganondagan, the village was systematically destroyed by the Denonville expedition in 1687, which forced the Seneca to rethink their settlement patterns and defensive strategies.

Tyendinaga

Location: Bay of Quinte | Era: 1784–Present

Tyendinaga was founded by Mohawk Loyalists led by Captain John Deseronto in the immediate aftermath of the American Revolution. Having lost their ancestral lands in the Mohawk Valley due to their alliance with the British Crown, these families crossed Lake Ontario to settle on the shores of the Bay of Quinte. Today, the Mohawks of the Bay of Quinte continue to maintain this territory as a vibrant center of Mohawk governance, language revitalization, and cultural heritage, representing the resilience of the Haudenosaunee people in the post-revolutionary era.

Unadilla

Location: Unadilla River | Era: 1700–1778

Unadilla was a thriving, multi-ethnic village situated on the Susquehanna River, primarily home to Mohawk and Oneida families. By the mid-1700s, it had become a significant supply depot and base of operations for Loyalist forces and Haudenosaunee war parties during the American Revolution. Because of its strategic importance in launching raids against the American frontier, the village was destroyed in a preemptive strike by American Continental forces in 1778. The destruction of Unadilla and its crops marked a major escalation in the frontier war.

Wyoming Valley

Location: Wilkes-Barre, PA | Era: 1740–1778

The Wyoming Valley was a fertile and highly contested region settled by various displaced tribes under the strict supervision of the Haudenosaunee. During the Revolutionary War, a combined force of Iroquois warriors and British Loyalists won a decisive and controversial victory here against American settlers. The ensuing "Wyoming Massacre" was used as a powerful piece of American propaganda to paint the Iroquois as "savages," providing the political justification for George Washington to order the total destruction of the Iroquois heartland the following year.

Cayuga Castle

Location: Cayuga Lake | Era: 1450–1779

Cayuga Castle, known natively as Goiogouen, was the primary political and spiritual capital of the Cayuga Nation. It consisted of three large, unfortified towns located along the eastern shore of Cayuga Lake. As the heart of Cayuga sovereignty, the settlement was surrounded by immense fields of corn and thriving fruit orchards that had been cultivated for generations. The entire complex was systematically razed by the Sullivan Expedition in 1779, forcing the Cayuga people to seek refuge with the Seneca or at British-held Fort Niagara.
